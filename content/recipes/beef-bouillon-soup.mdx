story: |
  Húsleves — clear beef broth — is the Hungarian Sunday opener,
  poured before the main course at any family lunch worth its
  tablecloth. The technique is the recipe: start cold, simmer
  patiently, skim relentlessly, never boil.

  Onion skins go in for colour; a parsnip and a small piece of
  celeriac for sweetness. A halved soft-boiled egg turns the broth
  into a meal in its own right. The clarity is the point — if the
  liquid clouds, you've cooked it too hard.

ingredients:
  - 1.5 kg beef shin or shank, on the bone
  - 1 large onion, halved, skin on
  - 3 carrots, peeled and cut in thirds
  - 2 stalks celery, cut in thirds
  - 1 small parsnip, peeled
  - 2 cloves garlic, smashed
  - 1 small bunch parsley, plus extra to garnish
  - 5 black peppercorns
  - 2 bay leaves
  - 2 tsp salt, plus more to taste
  - 3 l cold water
  - 4 eggs
instructions:
  - Put the beef in a tall pot and cover with the cold water. Bring slowly to a simmer, skimming off the grey foam that rises to the top — do this patiently for about ten minutes.
  - Add the onion, carrots, celery, parsnip, garlic, parsley stalks, peppercorns, bay leaves, and salt.
  - Lower the heat so the surface barely trembles. Cover loosely and cook for 2½ hours, never letting it boil hard.
  - 'About 10 minutes before serving, soft-boil the eggs: lower them into simmering water for 6 minutes, then peel under cold running water.'
  - Strain the broth through a fine sieve into clean bowls. Slice the meat thinly and divide between the bowls along with a few pieces of carrot.
  - Halve the soft eggs and float one over each portion. Shower with chopped parsley and adjust the salt at the table.
---

Clear, deep beef broth — Hungarian húsleves in its plainest form. A halved soft egg turns it into a meal. The secret is a low, lazy simmer; if it boils, the broth clouds.
