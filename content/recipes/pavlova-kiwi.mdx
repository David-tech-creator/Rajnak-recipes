story: |
  Pavlova is the dessert Australia and New Zealand still argue
  over — both claim invention in the 1920s, after Russian
  ballerina Anna Pavlova toured the Antipodes. The name stuck;
  the parentage is still in dispute. New Zealand has the earlier
  printed recipes; Australia has the louder voice.

  What makes a pavlova not a meringue is the soft, marshmallow
  centre: cornflour (or potato starch) and a splash of vinegar
  stabilise the egg whites so the inside stays cloud-like while
  the outside crisps. The slow oven and the long, undisturbed
  cool-down — door slightly open, ideally overnight — are what
  prevent the cracks and weeping.

  Topped with cream and tart fruit (kiwi, passionfruit, berries)
  the morning of serving. Never before — the meringue dissolves.

ingredients:
  - 6 egg whites, at room temperature
  - 3 dl caster sugar
  - 1.5 tsp white wine vinegar
  - 1 tbsp potato starch (or cornflour)
  - 1.5 tsp vanilla sugar
  - 3 dl double cream
  - 1 tbsp icing sugar
  - 4 ripe kiwis, peeled and sliced
instructions:
  - Heat the oven to 130 °C and line a baking tray with parchment, marking a 22-cm circle on it.
  - Whisk the egg whites in a spotlessly clean bowl until they hold stiff peaks.
  - Add the caster sugar one tablespoon at a time, whisking on high speed, until the meringue is thick, glossy, and holds firm peaks — about 5 minutes.
  - Whisk in the vinegar, then gently fold in the potato starch and vanilla sugar with a spatula.
  - Spread the meringue onto the parchment within the marked circle, building up the sides slightly higher to form a shallow nest.
  - Bake for 1 hour 30 minutes, then turn off the oven and leave the meringue inside, door slightly open, until completely cool — ideally overnight.
  - Whip the double cream with the icing sugar to soft peaks. Just before serving, pile the cream onto the meringue and top with sliced kiwi.
---

A classic pavlova: crisp on the outside, marshmallow-soft inside, crowned with cool cream and tart green kiwi. Don't assemble until the moment you serve, or the meringue weeps.
