story: |
  Glögg is the Swedish drink of Advent — the four weeks leading to
  Christmas — drunk from small cups with almonds and raisins at the
  bottom, alongside pepparkakor. The tradition runs across Scandinavia:
  glögg in Sweden and Norway, gløgg in Denmark, glögi in Finland, the
  same idea everywhere.

  The recipe is medieval in spirit (spiced wine to mask poor wine has
  been around since the Romans) but the modern Swedish version traces
  to the 17th century, when sugar, almonds, raisins, and the spice
  trade made it possible. Never let it boil — the alcohol leaves with
  the steam. Steep at a whisper just below simmer, ladle hot.
featured: false
ingredients:
  - 1 bottle (750 ml) full-bodied red wine
  - 2 dl vodka or aquavit
  - 1.5 dl granulated sugar
  - 10 whole cloves
  - 2 cinnamon sticks
  - 1 piece dried ginger (or 2-cm fresh ginger, sliced)
  - 1 piece dried orange peel (or peel of 1 orange)
  - 5 green cardamom pods, crushed
  - 100 g blanched almonds
  - 100 g raisins
instructions:
  - In a clean glass jar, combine the vodka with the cloves, cinnamon sticks, ginger, orange peel, and cardamom. Seal and leave at room temperature for 24 hours (or up to a week) to infuse.
  - Pour the spiced spirit into a heavy saucepan through a strainer, pressing on the spices. Add the wine and sugar.
  - Heat gently over low heat, stirring until the sugar dissolves. Do not let it boil — that drives off the alcohol.
  - Keep just below a simmer for 20–30 minutes so the flavours marry.
  - Drop a small handful of almonds and raisins into the bottom of each glögg cup.
  - Ladle the hot glögg over the top and serve at once, with pepparkakor on the side.
---

The drink of Swedish Advent — every household has a slightly different recipe, and everyone's grandmother makes the best. Start it the day before so the spices have time to do their work.
