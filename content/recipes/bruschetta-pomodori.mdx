story: |
  Bruschetta — from the Roman bruscare, "to roast over coals" — is the
  oldest Italian antipasto, born as a way to use up day-old bread by
  toasting it over an open fire. The Tuscan version with chopped
  tomatoes belongs to August, when the vines are heavy and the basil
  is everywhere.

  Pronounced bru-SKET-ta, not bru-SHET-ta. Everything depends on the
  tomatoes: full of sun, full of flavour, peeled if they're worth the
  trouble. The warm-toast garlic-rub is the move that turns four
  ingredients into something memorable.
featured: false
kitchenNote: 'Rub the warm toast with raw garlic.'
signoff: 'Buon appetito.'
ingredients:
  - 8-10 large ripe red tomatoes, full of flavour
  - 18 fresh basil leaves
  - 1.5 dl extra-virgin olive oil
  - 1 clove garlic, halved
  - 6 large slices Italian country bread
  - Sea salt
  - Freshly ground black pepper
instructions:
  - Score a small cross in the base of each tomato, dunk in boiling water for 15 seconds, then into iced water. The skins should slip off easily.
  - Chop the peeled tomato flesh coarsely and drop into a bowl, leaving most of the seeds and watery bits behind.
  - Shred half the basil leaves and add to the tomatoes with the olive oil. Stir, season with salt and pepper, and let stand 10 minutes.
  - Toast the bread golden brown — either in the oven, under a grill, or on a griddle.
  - Rub each warm toast on one side with the cut clove of garlic and sprinkle lightly with salt.
  - Spoon the chopped tomato evenly over the bread slices, letting the juices soak in.
  - Garnish with the remaining whole basil leaves and a final drizzle of olive oil. Serve immediately.
---

The whole dish depends on the tomatoes — Tuscan summer ones if you can; otherwise the best you can find. Basil can be swapped for parsley or shredded rocket. Eat with your hands, sitting outside, with the juice running down your wrist.
