story: |
  Gaeng phet — Thai red curry — gets its colour from dried red
  chillies pounded with lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime, garlic
  and shallot into a paste called nam phrik gaeng phet. Making
  the paste from scratch is a half-day affair; most Thai cooks
  buy it, and a good jarred paste (Mae Ploy, Maesri) gets
  startlingly close to a market stall.

  The defining technique is frying the paste in coconut cream
  until "the oil breaks" — when the cream separates and the
  paste sizzles in its own oil, sharpening every flavour. Add the
  coconut milk before this happens and you get soup; let it
  break first and you get curry.

ingredients:
  - 600 g chicken thigh fillets, sliced
  - 3 tbsp Thai red curry paste
  - 400 ml coconut milk
  - 2 dl chicken stock
  - 2 tbsp fish sauce
  - 1 tbsp brown sugar (palm sugar if you have it)
  - 4 kaffir lime leaves (optional)
  - 4 heads pak choi, halved lengthways
  - 2 tbsp neutral oil
  - 1 red chilli, sliced
  - Juice of 1 lime
  - 300 g jasmine rice
  - A small handful Thai basil or coriander, to serve
instructions:
  - Rinse the rice until the water runs clear, then cook with 6 dl water and a pinch of salt: bring to a boil, reduce to low, cover, and simmer for 12 minutes. Leave covered off the heat.
  - Heat the oil in a wok or wide pan over medium-high heat. Add the red curry paste and fry for 1 minute, until aromatic.
  - Stir in 1 dl of the coconut milk and let it bubble until the oil splits out — this is what gives the curry its colour.
  - Add the chicken and stir to coat. Cook for 3 minutes, until just turning opaque.
  - Pour in the rest of the coconut milk, the stock, fish sauce, sugar, and lime leaves. Simmer gently for 8 minutes, until the chicken is tender.
  - Slip the pak choi halves into the curry, cut-side down, and cook for 3 minutes, until the stems are just tender.
  - Squeeze in the lime juice, taste, adjust with more fish sauce or sugar if needed. Serve over rice, topped with red chilli and Thai basil.
---

A fast, fragrant Thai curry that leans on a good paste — Mae Ploy or Maesri if you can find them. The pak choi goes in at the end so the stems keep their crunch.
