story: |
  Fondue chinoise is a Swiss winter institution — despite the name,
  it has nothing to do with China. The dish was invented in
  Switzerland in the 1950s as a thinner-broth cousin to fondue
  bourguignonne (oil-poached beef), drawing very loose inspiration
  from Mongolian and Chinese hot-pot traditions.

  It belongs to Christmas Eve and New Year's, served at the table with
  fondue forks, three sauces, and a Beilage of fruit, nuts, and salad.
  Each guest cooks their own — a minute or so for beef, a touch longer
  for chicken. The broth gets richer with every dipping, and the small
  bowl of broth at the end is the best part of the meal.

ingredients:
  - 800 g mixed meats (beef sirloin, chicken breast, pork tenderloin), all cut into 2-cm cubes
  - 200 g raw peeled prawns
  - 1.5 l good beef or chicken stock
  - 1 sachet Chinese fondue spice mix
  - 1 stalk lemongrass, bashed
  - 3 slices ginger
  - 2 spring onions, halved
  - "Sauces:"
  - 2 dl aioli (mayo with 2 crushed garlic cloves)
  - 1 ripe avocado mashed with 1 dl crème fraîche, juice of ½ lemon, salt
  - 2 dl mayo mixed with 1 tbsp Tabasco, 1 tbsp ketchup, 1 tsp Worcestershire (devil sauce)
  - "Fun salad accessories:"
  - 1 ripe pear, sliced
  - 1 ripe avocado, sliced
  - 200 g cottage cheese (keso)
  - 1 little gem lettuce, leaves separated
  - Walnuts and pomegranate seeds
instructions:
  - Two hours before dinner, slice all the meats into neat 2-cm cubes and arrange on a chilled platter, covered, in the fridge. Do the same with the prawns.
  - Make the three sauces and spoon into small bowls — they only improve as they sit.
  - 'Compose the "fun" salad on a wide plate: lettuce leaves as a base, sliced pear and avocado fanned out, cottage cheese in the centre, walnuts and pomegranate scattered over.'
  - Pour the stock into a fondue pot. Add the spice mix, lemongrass, ginger, and spring onions. Bring to a steady simmer at the table.
  - Each diner spears a piece of meat or prawn on a long fork and lowers it into the simmering broth — about 90 seconds for chicken or pork, 60 for beef, 45 for prawn.
  - Eat hot, dipping each piece into one of the three sauces.
  - At the end, ladle the now-deeply-flavoured broth into small bowls — the best part of the meal.
---

The Christmas Eve or New Year fondue at our house. Everyone cooks their own at the table, and the salad on the side is the lighter counter-balance. Use proper sturdy fondue forks; toothpicks don't survive.
