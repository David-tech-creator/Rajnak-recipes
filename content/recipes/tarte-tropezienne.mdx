story: |
  La tarte tropézienne was invented in 1955 in Saint-Tropez by
  Alexandre Micka, a Polish baker who had brought his grandmother's
  brioche recipe to the south of France. The film crew shooting "Et
  Dieu créa la femme" ate at his bakery, and Brigitte Bardot, then
  twenty-one, suggested he give the cake a name. He registered "La
  Tarte Tropézienne" as a trademark in 1955.

  The cake is a split brioche scattered with pearl sugar, filled with a
  mousseline — pastry cream lightened with whipped cream and scented
  with orange flower water. The version of the cream is still a guarded
  secret at Maison Micka, but the contour is well-known: half crème
  pâtissière, half whipped cream, a generous splash of orange flower
  water.

ingredients:
  - 300 g plain flour
  - 50 g caster sugar
  - 1 tsp salt
  - 7 g instant yeast
  - 3 large eggs
  - 80 ml whole milk, warm
  - 150 g unsalted butter, softened
  - 1 egg yolk, for the wash
  - 50 g pearl sugar (sucre en grains)
  - 500 ml whole milk
  - 1 vanilla pod, split
  - 5 egg yolks
  - 100 g caster sugar
  - 40 g cornflour
  - 250 ml whipping cream
  - 2 tbsp orange flower water
instructions:
  - For the brioche, combine flour, sugar, salt, and yeast in a stand mixer. Add the eggs and warm milk and knead with the dough hook for 8 minutes, until smooth and elastic. Add the soft butter a tablespoon at a time, kneading until fully incorporated and the dough is shiny. Cover and let rise 1.5 hours until doubled.
  - Press the dough into a flat disc about 22 cm wide on a parchment-lined baking sheet. Cover loosely and prove another 45 minutes.
  - Heat the oven to 180 °C. Brush the brioche with egg yolk, scatter pearl sugar generously over the top, and bake 18 to 22 minutes until deep golden. Cool completely.
  - For the cream, heat the milk with the split vanilla pod until just steaming. Whisk the 5 yolks with the sugar and cornflour until pale, pour the hot milk over while whisking, then return to the pan and cook over medium heat, whisking constantly, until thick and bubbling. Press cling film onto the surface and chill thoroughly.
  - Whip the cream to firm peaks. Whisk the chilled pastry cream smooth, then fold in the whipped cream and orange flower water — this is the famous mousseline.
  - Slice the cooled brioche horizontally in half. Pipe a thick, generous layer of cream over the bottom half, set the sugared top in place, and chill at least an hour before slicing.

The Saint-Tropez cake invented in 1955 by a Polish baker for Brigitte Bardot. A buttery brioche split and filled with a cloud of orange-blossom cream — the taste of a French summer.
