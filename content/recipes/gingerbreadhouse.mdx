story: |
  Pepparkakshus — the gingerbread house — is the long-form Advent
  project of Swedish December. The dough goes back to medieval
  pepparkakor, which arrived in Sweden via German nuns in the 14th
  century; the house tradition is younger, popularised in the late 19th
  century alongside the Christmas tree.

  The dough rests overnight to deepen flavour and roll more easily. The
  pieces bake darker than you think they should — they need to be
  fully crisp or the walls slump. Royal icing is the structural glue
  and the decorative trim. Build walls first, let them dry an hour,
  then roof. By Boxing Day the house is for breaking and eating.

ingredients:
  - 200 g butter
  - 2 dl golden syrup (or dark corn syrup)
  - 2 dl dark muscovado sugar
  - 1 tbsp ground cinnamon
  - 1 tbsp ground ginger
  - 1 tbsp ground cloves
  - 1 tbsp ground cardamom
  - 1 tsp baking soda
  - 1 dl water
  - 800 g plain flour, plus more for rolling
  - 3 egg whites
  - 600 g icing sugar
  - 1 tsp lemon juice
  - Sweets and decorations of choice
instructions:
  - Melt the butter, syrup, sugar and spices in a saucepan until the sugar dissolves. Stir in the baking soda and water. Cool to lukewarm.
  - Work in the flour to a smooth dough. Wrap and chill overnight.
  - Heat the oven to 200°C. Roll the dough out to 3-mm thickness on a floured surface. Using paper templates, cut the walls, roof panels, and floor for your house.
  - Transfer the pieces to baking trays lined with parchment and bake for 10–12 minutes, until firm and deeply coloured. Cool completely on a rack — they must be cold and crisp before you build.
  - Whisk the egg whites with the icing sugar and lemon juice to a stiff, glossy royal icing.
  - Assemble the house by piping icing along each edge and pressing the pieces together. Hold for a minute until set. Build the walls first, let them dry, then add the roof. Decorate with sweets.
---

The Swedish Advent project — the dough is made one evening, the pieces baked the next, and the house decorated on a third with everyone in the kitchen. Eat by mid-January or save it for next year's mantel.
