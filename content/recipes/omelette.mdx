  The French omelette is the chef-school test: three eggs, butter,
  salt, and a non-stick pan, and yet it takes years to get right. A
  proper French omelette is pale yellow, almost no colour on the
  outside, folded into a soft cylinder, and baveuse — runny — in the
  middle. The British version (drier, browner, often loaded with
  fillings) is a different dish entirely.

  Don't overbeat the eggs; just break the yolks. Medium-high heat,
  butter that foams but doesn't brown, and the nerve to take it off
  while the top is still glossy. Eat immediately. It waits for nobody.

ingredients:
  - 3 large eggs, fresh
  - 1 tbsp whole milk or water
  - 0.25 tsp salt
  - A grind of black pepper
  - 1 tbsp butter
  - 'Optional fillings:'
  - 2 tbsp grated Gruyère or cheddar
  - 1 tbsp chopped chives, parsley, or tarragon
  - 50 g sautéed mushrooms or diced ham
instructions:
  - Crack the eggs into a bowl. Add the milk or water, salt, and pepper. Beat with a fork just until the yolks and whites are combined — don't overbeat.
  - Heat a 20-cm non-stick pan over medium-high heat. Drop in the butter and swirl until it foams but does not brown.
  - Pour in the eggs. Let them set for five seconds, then start drawing the cooked edges into the centre with a spatula, tilting the pan so the raw egg runs into the gaps.
  - When the omelette is almost set but still a little wet on top (about 90 seconds in), scatter your filling over one half.
  - Tilt the pan and fold the empty half over the filling. Slide onto a warm plate.
  - Finish with chopped herbs and another grind of pepper. Eat immediately.

The five-minute breakfast or quick supper — eggs, butter, the right pan, and a little nerve to take it off before it's fully set. A French baveuse middle is the goal.
