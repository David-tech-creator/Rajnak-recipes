story: |
  Våffeldagen, Sweden's official waffle day, falls on the 25th of March
  — but only by linguistic accident. The day is the Feast of the
  Annunciation, Vårfrudagen ("Our Lady's Day"), and in spoken Swedish
  the words slurred together into "våffeldagen," waffle day. The food
  followed the joke.

  Swedish waffles are not the thick Belgian kind. They are thin,
  crisp, and heart-shaped, baked in a special iron, and folded in cold
  whipped cream into the batter at the last moment for the shatter.
  Eaten with jam — strawberry, raspberry, or for the proper north,
  hjortronsylt (cloudberry jam) — and a cloud of whipped cream.

ingredients:
  - 3 dl plain flour
  - 5 dl cold water
  - 1.5 dl cold whipping cream
  - 100 g butter, melted and cooled
  - 0.5 tsp salt
  - 1 tsp baking powder
  - 1 tsp sugar
  - Butter for the iron
  - 3 dl whipping cream, for serving
  - 1 jar good strawberry or cloudberry jam (hjortronsylt)
instructions:
  - Whisk the flour with the cold water in a large bowl until completely smooth and lump-free.
  - Stir in the salt, sugar, baking powder, and melted butter.
  - Whip the 1.5 dl cream to soft peaks and fold gently into the batter — this is what makes the waffles light and crisp.
  - Heat the waffle iron (a Swedish heart-shaped iron if you have one) and brush lightly with butter. Pour in just enough batter to fill, close, and cook until deep golden and crisp, about 3 minutes.
  - Whip the remaining 3 dl cream to soft peaks. Serve the waffles straight off the iron with a spoonful of jam and a cloud of whipped cream.
---

The Swedish waffle day, våffeldagen, falls on 25 March — but these are good any rainy afternoon. The cold cream folded in at the end gives them their unmistakable shatter.
