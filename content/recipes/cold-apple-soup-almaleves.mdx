story: |
  Hideg almaleves — cold apple soup — is part of a small but distinct
  Hungarian tradition of fruit-based cold soups served as a summer
  starter. Hideg meggyleves (sour cherry) is the most famous; apple,
  rhubarb, and elderberry versions appear in regional cookbooks
  alongside it.

  The habarás is the technique that defines the dish: a sour-cream-
  and-flour slurry tempered with hot liquid, then whisked back into
  the pot to thicken without curdling. It's the Hungarian alternative
  to a roux, and once you understand it, you understand half of
  Hungarian home cooking.
featured: false
kitchenNote: 'Temper the sour cream or it splits.'
signoff: 'Jó étvágyat.'
ingredients:
  - 6 tart apples (Granny Smith or Boskoop), peeled, cored, and diced
  - 1.2 l water
  - 1 cinnamon stick
  - 3 whole cloves
  - Zest of ½ lemon
  - 2 tbsp sugar (more to taste)
  - Pinch of salt
  - 2 dl sour cream (tejföl)
  - 1 tbsp plain flour
  - 1 egg yolk
  - 1 tbsp lemon juice
  - Ground cinnamon, to garnish
instructions:
  - Put the diced apples, water, cinnamon stick, cloves, lemon zest, sugar, and salt in a pot. Bring to a gentle simmer.
  - Cook for 15-20 minutes, until the apples are very soft. Fish out the cinnamon stick and cloves.
  - In a bowl, whisk the flour smoothly into the sour cream, then beat in the egg yolk and lemon juice. This is the habarás — Hungary's classic sour cream thickener.
  - Ladle a few spoons of the hot apple liquid into the sour cream mixture, whisking constantly, to temper it.
  - Tip the tempered habarás back into the pot, stirring. Cook on low heat for 3 more minutes — do not boil, or the egg will curdle.
  - Taste for sweetness and tartness. Adjust sugar and lemon to your liking.
  - Cool, then chill thoroughly for at least 2 hours.
  - Serve cold in bowls, dusted with cinnamon.
---

A Hungarian summer-day soup — sweet-sour, creamy with sour cream, eaten cold like gazpacho but apple-scented. The habarás is the technique; treat it gently or the egg curdles. Best the day after.
