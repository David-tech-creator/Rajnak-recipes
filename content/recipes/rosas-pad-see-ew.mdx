story: |
  Pad see ew — literally "stir-fried with soy sauce" — is Thai
  street-corner comfort food, cousin to pad thai but darker,
  smokier, less sweet. Wide flat rice noodles (sen yai) get
  charred against the wok, dark soy glazes them mahogany, white
  pepper finishes hot and prickly.

  The dish lives or dies by wok hei — the breath of the wok, the
  smoky aroma that comes only from a screaming-hot pan and a
  single portion at a time. Doubling the recipe never works: the
  food cools the wok, the noodles steam, the char never comes.
  Cook one plate, eat one plate, then cook the next.

ingredients:
  - 2 tbsp vegetable oil
  - 1 tbsp crushed garlic
  - 1 egg
  - 125 g sirloin steak, thinly sliced
  - 150 g thick, flat fresh rice noodles (or dried noodles, soaked until soft)
  - 1 tbsp oyster sauce
  - 1 tbsp dark soy sauce
  - 1 large pinch white pepper, plus more to serve
  - 0.5 tbsp fish sauce (optional)
  - 1 tsp granulated sugar
  - 1 large handful Chinese kale (gai lan), roughly chopped — or any leafy green
  - 1 small handful beansprouts (optional)
instructions:
  - Heat the oil in a wok over medium-high heat. When hot, add the garlic and stir-fry until fragrant — about 15 seconds.
  - Add the sliced beef and stir-fry until just cooked through. Push the meat to the side and crack in the egg, scrambling lightly before mixing back through.
  - Add the noodles and toss well to combine. Pour in the oyster sauce and dark soy and stir-fry hard for 1 minute, letting the noodles char in places.
  - Add the white pepper, fish sauce, and sugar. Toss until each strand is glossy and the sauce has reduced.
  - Throw in the chopped kale and stir-fry for another minute, just until the leaves wilt and the stems are tender-crisp.
  - Tip onto a plate, scatter with beansprouts if using, and finish with a generous shower of white pepper.

Rosa's Thai Café's signature noodle dish — comfort food from every street corner of Thailand. The distinctive flavour comes from charred noodles and a heavy hand with white pepper. Cook one portion at a time so the wok stays screaming hot.
