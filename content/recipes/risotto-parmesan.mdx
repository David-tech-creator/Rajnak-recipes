story: |
  Risotto alla parmigiana — also called risotto in bianco — is
  the mother risotto of the Po valley, the blank canvas from
  which every other version starts. No saffron (that's milanese),
  no seafood, no vegetables. Just rice, stock, butter, parmesan.

  The technique is everything. Toast the rice until each grain is
  glossy (tostatura); deglaze with wine; add hot stock one ladle
  at a time, stirring gently to release starch; finish off the
  heat with cold butter and grated parmesan, beaten in hard
  (mantecatura) until the surface glistens and ripples like a
  wave when the pan is shaken — the test the Italians call
  all'onda.

  Carnaroli is the rice of choice over arborio: longer grains,
  more starch, less likely to blow out.

ingredients:
  - 350 g carnaroli or arborio rice
  - 1 shallot, finely chopped
  - 1 small clove garlic, finely chopped
  - 1 dl dry white wine
  - 1.2 l hot chicken or vegetable stock
  - 50 g unsalted butter, divided
  - 2 tbsp olive oil
  - 80 g parmesan, finely grated, plus more to serve
  - Salt and white pepper, to taste
  - 1 small handful flat-leaf parsley (optional), finely chopped
instructions:
  - Keep the stock hot in a small saucepan on a back burner.
  - Heat the olive oil and half the butter in a heavy, wide pan over medium heat. Add the shallot and a pinch of salt and sweat for 4 minutes, until soft but not coloured. Add the garlic for the last 30 seconds.
  - Tip in the rice and stir for 2 minutes, until each grain is glossy and faintly translucent at the edges.
  - Pour in the wine and let it bubble away, stirring, until almost gone.
  - Add a ladle of hot stock and stir gently until absorbed. Continue ladling and stirring, one ladle at a time, for about 18 minutes, until the rice is creamy with a slight bite at the centre.
  - Pull off the heat. Beat in the remaining butter and the parmesan vigorously — this is the mantecatura, what makes it shiny.
  - Cover for 2 minutes to rest, then taste, season, and serve in shallow bowls with extra parmesan and a sprinkle of parsley.

The base risotto every other risotto starts from — risotto alla parmigiana. Get this right and the rest is just choosing a vegetable. Use real stock; it matters.
